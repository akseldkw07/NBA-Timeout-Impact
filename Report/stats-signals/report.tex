\title{Detecting Timeout Effects in the NBA via the Generalized Likelihood Ratio Test}

\author{\name Aksel Kretsinger-Walters \email adk2164@columbia.edu \\
		\addr Department of Computer Science\\
		Columbia University\\
New York, NY 10027, USA}

\editor{}

\maketitle

\begin{abstract}%
		What impact do NBA timeouts have on game outcomes? We pose that
		question as a battery of one-sample Gaussian signal-detection problems,
		one per impact metric, timeout type, and game-state slice. For three
		impact metrics derived from the cdnnba 2020--2025 play-by-play feed
		-- excess net points, excess points-per-possession (PPP), and
		win-probability added -- we test $H_0\!:\mu = 0$ against
		$H_1\!:\mu \neq 0$ using the generalized likelihood ratio statistic
		$G = -2\log\Lambda$, which under $H_0$ is asymptotically $\chi^2_1$.
		The tests are run separately for coach full timeouts, coach
		challenges, and inferred mandatory television timeouts, and within
		each subtype we slice by score margin, streak, game phase, home
		court, and regular season vs.\ playoffs. The point-differential
		metric is null in the aggregate but surfaces a clean regression-to-mean
		pattern once we stratify by margin or streak. The win-probability
		metric, in contrast, rejects $H_0$ by enormous margins: full coach
		timeouts cost the calling team about $3.9$ percentage points of WP
		across a $\pm 6$ possession window, challenges add about $1.6$ pp,
		and television timeouts cost $1.3$ pp -- broadly aligned with the
		prior causal literature. Of $163$ stratified GLRTs, $69$ reject at
		$\alpha = 0.05$ ($42.3\%$), about $8\times$ what we would expect if
		the null held everywhere.
\end{abstract}

\begin{keywords}
		Generalized likelihood ratio test, signal detection, sports analytics, NBA,
		timeouts, win probability
\end{keywords}

\section{Problem Statement}
\label{sec:problem}

For every coach-called timeout, the NBA play-by-play feed records the
state of the game on either side of the stoppage. Whether anything in
those few possessions actually traces back to the timeout itself is
genuinely contested
\citep{assis2020stop,gibbs2021causal,weimer2023causal,qiu2025interrupt}.
Rather than try to estimate a causal effect directly, we ask a narrower
question: for each of three per-event impact metrics, taken within a
fixed slice of game state, is the population mean zero?

Let $X_1,\dots,X_n$ be the impact-metric values across timeouts within
some stratum -- say, full coach timeouts called while leading by 6--15
points -- and assume $X_i$ iid $\mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$ with both
parameters unknown. We test
\begin{equation}
		H_0\!: \mu = 0 \qquad \text{vs.} \qquad H_1\!: \mu \neq 0.
		\label{eq:hyp}
\end{equation}
A rejection means the timeout type, conditioned on the stratum, is
associated with a non-zero shift in the metric.

\section{Data and Metrics}
\label{sec:data}

Our data is the cdnnba enriched play-by-play feed for the 2020--2025
seasons (regular season and playoffs): $4{,}183{,}347$ rows across
$7{,}457$ games and $1{,}480{,}697$ possessions. The feed does not
label mandatory TV timeouts, so we inject them with a deterministic
rulebook procedure and validate the injection against the explicitly
labeled 2013--2016 nbastatsv3 feed, where it scores F1 between $0.88$
and $0.92$. After injection the corpus contains $74{,}236$ \texttt{full}
coach timeouts, $1{,}396$ \texttt{challenge} events, and $14{,}538$
inferred \texttt{official\_inferred} (TV) timeouts.

For each timeout event we look at a window of $W=6$ possessions before and
$W$ after, and compute three metrics from the calling team's perspective.
\begin{description}
		\item[Q1.~\texttt{excess\_net\_post}] Calling-team net points (for $-$ against)
				in the post window, minus the season-PPP-gap expectation
				$(\mathrm{PPP}_{\text{calling}} - \mathrm{PPP}_{\text{opponent}})\cdot W/2$.
				$\mu = 0$ corresponds to the calling team performing at the season expectation.
		\item[Q2.~\texttt{excess\_ppp\_for\_post}] Calling-team PPP in the post window
				minus that team's full-season PPP. $\mu = 0$ means the team scores at
				season pace.
		\item[Q3.~\texttt{wp\_added\_calling}] Change in calling-team win probability
				across the $\pm W$ possession window, where $\Pr(\text{home wins})$ is the
				output of a logistic-regression model trained on possession-start snapshots
				with the ten features score margin, period, seconds remaining,
				clutch indicator, possessing-team-is-home indicator, streak,
				playoff indicator, home win-percentage, away win-percentage, and the
				home-minus-away win-percentage difference. $\mu = 0$ means
				the window had no net WP movement for the calling team.
\end{description}
The WP model achieves AUC $0.844$ and Brier score $0.161$ on a
held-out-by-game test set ($n=59{,}860$).

For each metric we run the GLRT separately for the three timeout subtypes
and stratify on five context columns from the project README:
\texttt{is\_home\_calling}, \texttt{IsPlayoff}, \texttt{time\_bucket}
($\text{Q1}, \text{Q2}, \text{Q3}, \text{Q4\_early}, \text{Q4\_clutch},
\text{OT}$), \texttt{margin\_bucket} (seven buckets from down-15 to up-15
from the calling team's perspective), and \texttt{streak\_bucket} (seven
buckets from a 10-pt opposing run to a 10-pt own run).

\section{Methods: GLRT Derivation}
\label{sec:method}

For $X_1,\dots,X_n$ iid $\mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$ the log-likelihood is
\[
		\log L(\mu, \sigma^2) = -\tfrac{n}{2}\log(2\pi\sigma^2) -
		\frac{1}{2\sigma^2}\sum_{i=1}^n (x_i - \mu)^2.
\]
Under $H_0$ ($\mu=0$) the MLE is $\hat\sigma^2_0 = \tfrac{1}{n}\sum x_i^2$;
under $H_1$ the MLEs are $\hat\mu_1 = \bar x$ and $\hat\sigma^2_1 =
\tfrac{1}{n}\sum (x_i - \bar x)^2$. The supremum log-likelihood under each
hypothesis evaluates to $-\tfrac{n}{2}(1 + \log(2\pi\hat\sigma^2_*))$, so
the generalized likelihood ratio is
\begin{equation}
		\Lambda \;=\; \frac{\sup_{\sigma^2} L(0, \sigma^2)}
		{\sup_{\mu, \sigma^2} L(\mu, \sigma^2)}
		\;=\; \Big(\frac{\hat\sigma^2_1}{\hat\sigma^2_0}\Big)^{n/2}.
		\label{eq:lambda}
\end{equation}
Using the identity $\hat\sigma^2_0 = \hat\sigma^2_1 + \bar x^2$ we obtain
the test statistic
\begin{equation}
		G \;=\; -2\log\Lambda \;=\; n\,\log\!\Big(1 + \frac{\bar x^2}{\hat\sigma^2_1}\Big),
		\label{eq:glrt}
\end{equation}
which by Wilks' theorem is asymptotically $\chi^2_1$ under $H_0$. We reject
$H_0$ at level $\alpha = 0.05$ when $G > \chi^2_{1, 0.95} \approx 3.841$.
Equivalently, with $s^2 = \tfrac{n}{n-1}\hat\sigma^2_1$ and the one-sample
$t$-statistic $t = \bar x / (s/\sqrt n)$,
\[
		G = n\,\log\!\left(1 + \frac{t^2}{n - 1}\right) \;\xrightarrow{n\to\infty}\; t^2,
\]
so the GLRT and the standard $t$-test are asymptotically equivalent;
we report both throughout for cross-checking.

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Aggregated GLRT Results}

$(\text{metric}, \text{subtype})$ pair pooled over the entire cdnnba era.

\paragraph{Q1.} Pooled across the league, Q1 is dead null: $G = 0.98$,
$p = 0.32$ for the most populous \texttt{full} subtype. This matches
the null result \citet{assis2020stop} report and is consistent with
plain regression to the mean. Timeouts are mostly called from a
deficit, so the gap between pre-timeout net
($\bar x_{\text{pre}} = -1.73$ pts) and post-timeout net
($\bar x_{\text{post}} = -0.02$ pts) gets absorbed by the season
baseline once we subtract the PPP gap.

\paragraph{Q2.} Stripping out the opponent's contribution helps. Q2
rejects $H_0$ for the \texttt{full} subtype ($+0.007$ PPP, $p = 0.008$)
and for the \texttt{official\_inferred} subtype ($+0.013$ PPP,
$p = 0.018$). The effects are small but real: roughly $0.7\%$ of a
possession's worth of scoring above season pace for coach timeouts, and
$1.3\%$ for TV timeouts.

\paragraph{Q3.} Q3 rejects $H_0$ for every subtype, by huge margins.
Full coach timeouts cost the calling team $3.9$ WP percentage points on
average across the $\pm 6$ possession window
($G = 1.09\!\times\!10^4$); challenges \emph{add} $1.6$ pp
($G = 34$, $p < 10^{-8}$); TV timeouts cost $1.3$ pp ($G = 245$). The
challenge sign is unsurprising -- challenges are filed when a team
thinks a call went against them, so the post-window typically reflects
a restored possession or extra free throws. The negative sign on full
coach timeouts is the more striking finding, and lines up with the
slightly negative ATT \citet{gibbs2021causal} reported via genetic
matching.

Figure~\ref{fig:overall-three} plots the GLRT statistics on a log scale
next to the sample mean and a $95\%$ confidence interval. The
orders-of-magnitude gap between Q1 and Q3 is most of the story here:
measuring impact in WP units rather than raw point swings amplifies the
statistical signal by several orders of magnitude.

\subsection{Stratified by Pre-Timeout Streak}

The streak bucket bins the calling team's signed streak; positive means
the calling team itself has been on the run. Figure~\ref{fig:q3-streak}
makes clear just how heterogeneous the WP signal is across that
dimension. Full timeouts called \emph{into} a $6$--$9$-point opposing
run cost the caller $-10.3$ pp of WP ($G = 12{,}992$, $n = 15{,}219$);
full timeouts called while the calling team is on its own
$6$--$9$-point run \emph{add} $+9.5$ pp ($G = 1{,}135$, $n = 1{,}470$).
The signed effects are roughly symmetric around streak $= 0$, and that
symmetry is the headline finding here: timeouts do not reverse momentum
within the post window. They ride it -- regardless of which team called
them.

\begin{figure}[t]
		\centering
		\includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{figures/q3_streak.jpg}
		\caption{Q3 WP added stratified by pre-timeout streak. Bold filled markers
		indicate strata where $H_0\!:\mu=0$ is rejected at $\alpha = 0.05$.}
		\label{fig:q3-streak}
\end{figure}

Applying the same stratification to Q1 (Figure~\ref{fig:q1-streak})
gives a much weaker version of the same picture, because the PPP-gap
baseline absorbs most of the signal. The direction still tracks:
down-streak timeouts produce small positive Q1 excesses, up-streak
timeouts produce small negative ones.

\subsection{Stratified by Score Margin}

Slicing on the calling team's score margin (Figure~\ref{fig:q1-margin})
recovers a clean regression-to-mean signal in Q1 that pooling erases.
Every down-by bucket for \texttt{full} timeouts shows positive Q1
excess (\texttt{down15+}: $+0.17$, $G = 24.4$; \texttt{down6-15}:
$+0.11$, $G = 24.9$); every up-by bucket shows negative Q1 excess
(\texttt{up15+}: $-0.26$, $G = 39.5$; \texttt{up6-15}: $-0.16$,
$G = 37.8$). The same monotone pattern persists, more faintly, for
inferred TV timeouts. The challenge subtype is too small to register a
margin signal at all.

		\centering
		\includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{figures/q1_margin.jpg}
		\caption{Q1 excess net post stratified by calling-team score margin. The
				left-to-right monotone trend is the regression-to-mean signature: trailing
				teams gain back roughly what they were missing, leading teams give some
		back.}
		\label{fig:q1-margin}

The corresponding Q3 margin stratification (Figure~\ref{fig:q3-margin})
tells a related story from the WP side. Full-timeout WP loss peaks in
moderately adverse situations (\texttt{down6-15}: $-6.1$ pp,
$G = 6{,}749$; \texttt{down1-5}: $-5.3$ pp, $G = 2{,}562$) and shrinks
toward zero in extreme leads or deficits, where the outcome of the game
is already mostly decided.

\subsection{Stratified by Game Phase}

Figure~\ref{fig:q3-time} breaks Q3 out by quarter, with Q4 split into
early ($> 5$ min remaining) and clutch ($\leq 5$ min). The full-timeout
WP cost is roughly flat across Q1--Q3 ($-4.0$ to $-4.5$ pp) and OT
($-5.3$ pp), but \emph{halves} in Q4-clutch, to $-2.0$ pp. Two
forces probably contribute to that drop: coaches are most deliberate
about timeout spending in clutch time, and the WP model itself is most
informative in those windows because each remaining possession carries
more leverage. TV timeouts behave similarly -- their WP cost
collapses to zero in Q4-clutch ($+0.0002$ pp, ns).

\end{figure}

\subsection{Pooled Distribution of Test Statistics}

Across four stratification schemes (overall, streak, margin, time) and
three impact metrics we ran $163$ stratified GLRTs in total, and $69$
of them reject $H_0$ at $\alpha = 0.05$ -- $42.3\%$, about $8\times$
the rate we would expect under a universal null. Figure~\ref{fig:null-dist}
plots the empirical distribution of $G$ values against its $\chi^2_1$
reference. The shift to the right is heavy, which is what you would
see if many of those rejections are genuine signal rather than chance.

		\centering

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

The three metrics behave so differently because each one packs the
noise into the null in a different way. Q1 cancels by construction in
the aggregate: the PPP-gap baseline is engineered so that
$\mathbb{E}[\,\bar X\,] = 0$ under no-effect. Signal only re-emerges
once we stratify on a context that the baseline does not already know
about. Q2 leaves the opponent's contribution in, which is why even the
aggregate test rejects weakly. Q3 is fundamentally different: WP is
non-linear in score margin, especially in the endgame, so a single
$\sim\!1$-point swing can move WP by an order of magnitude more in
clutch time than at tip-off. That is why even tiny mean shifts in WP
produce GLRT statistics in the thousands.

The single clearest finding in the whole report is the signed-WP
picture in Figure~\ref{fig:q3-streak}: \emph{timeouts ride momentum,
they do not reverse it}. Whether that reflects a genuine causal failure
of the timeout (the calling team was hot, and would have stayed hot
anyway) or selection bias (coaches call timeouts in specific,
predictable game states) is something between-group GLRTs cannot
answer on their own. What our tests do is pin down the \emph{observed}
pattern with high precision. Recovering the \emph{cause} of that
pattern requires matching, mediator analysis, or an instrument in the
spirit of \citet{weimer2023causal}.

\paragraph{Limitations.} A few worth flagging. First, this investigation seeks to understand the statistical
properties of timeout impacts via GLRT. It is \textbf{not} a causal inference study. It would be
misleading to interpret the results as causal effects, when they're explicitly statistical observations.
Second, the GLRT relies on the underlying metric being Gaussian. Q1 and Q2 are noisy averages
of point counts and are approximately Gaussian by CLT; Q3 is the output
of a logistic model and is bounded in $[-1, 1]$, so the Gaussian
assumption breaks near the endpoints. In practice the $t$-test and
GLRT $p$-values agree to four decimal places throughout, which is
reassuring, but a non-parametric permutation test would be a more
rigorous check. Third, the $163$ tests are not independent -- many
events appear in multiple stratifications -- so a
Bonferroni or Benjamini--Hochberg correction would over-correct here;
we report uncorrected $p$-values. Finally, the TV-timeout cohort is
rulebook-inferred rather than ground-truth labeled, with
F1 $\approx 0.9$ against the validation era, so the
\texttt{official\_inferred} effect sizes should be read as a lower
bound on the true mandatory-timeout effect.

\paragraph{Future work.} The cleanest extension is to swap the
unconditional null in Equation~\ref{eq:hyp} for a counterfactual null
that conditions on game state. The recipe: for each timeout, find a
matched non-timeout possession in the same game, take the paired
difference of the impact metric, and run the GLRT on those differences.
That turns the GLRT into a within-pair causal estimator and keeps both
the variance reduction of matching and the asymptotic distribution
theory of the test.
